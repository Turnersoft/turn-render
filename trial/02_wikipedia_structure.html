In mathematics, a group is a set equipped with an operation that combines any two of its elements to form a third element in such a way that four conditions called group axioms are satisfied, namely closure, associativity, identity and invertibility.

Groups are fundamental in abstract algebra: other well-known algebraic structures, such as rings, fields, and vector spaces, can all be seen as groups equipped with additional operations and axioms. Groups recur throughout mathematics, and the methods of group theory have influenced many parts of algebra.

Definition

Definition. A group is an ordered pair (G, ∗), where G is a set and ∗ is a binary operation on G, satisfying the following axioms:

  1. Closure: For all a, b ∈ G, the result of the operation a ∗ b is also in G.
  2. Associativity: For all a, b, c ∈ G, (a ∗ b) ∗ c = a ∗ (b ∗ c).
  3. Identity element: There exists an element e ∈ G such that, for every element a ∈ G, the equation e ∗ a = a ∗ e = a holds.
  4. Inverse element: For each a ∈ G, there exists an element b ∈ G such that a ∗ b = b ∗ a = e, where e is the identity element.

The result of an operation may be denoted as ab or a + b, rather than a ∗ b, depending on the context. The identity element is often denoted 1 or e for multiplicative groups, and 0 for additive groups.

Examples

Integers under addition

The set of integers ℤ under the operation of addition forms a group. The identity element is 0, and the inverse of an integer n is −n.

Symmetric groups

For any positive integer n, the symmetric group S_n consists of all permutations of a set with n elements. The group operation is function composition.

Matrix groups

The general linear group GL(n, ℝ) consists of all invertible n × n matrices with real entries, under matrix multiplication.

Basic properties

Uniqueness of identity and inverses. The identity element of a group is unique. Similarly, every element has a unique inverse.

This and other basic properties follow from the group axioms. For instance, the cancellation laws hold: if ab = ac, then b = c.

Subgroups

A subgroup of a group G is a subset H of G that is itself a group under the same operation. Important examples include:

  • Cyclic subgroups
  • Normal subgroups
  • Sylow subgroups

Group homomorphisms

A group homomorphism is a function f: G → H between two groups that preserves the group operation: f(ab) = f(a)f(b) for all elements a, b ∈ G.
